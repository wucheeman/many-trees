Preservation of this historical property could be of significant interest to all Americans, and especially African-Americans

Howard Lee, former mayor, Chapel Hill, NC

Many Trees is a historic farm in Durham, Carolina. It offers a significant opportunity to preserve part of North Carolina's history as well as wildlife habitat. Located in the midst of a rapidly growing area, Many Trees could provide excellent educational facilities for teaching about plantation slavery, local plants and animals, and even organic farming. A portion of the site could be developed as a pocket neighborhood as well. Many Trees's current owner is seeking a buyer or government agency that might purchase the site or accept some or all of it in perpetuity. Story of the Farm

Native Americans:  Prior to the arrival of white settlers in the 1750s peoples of the Shakori, Eno, and Sissipahaw tribes lived in the area. They formed a part of a larger trade network, as evidenced by the many arrowheads and other stone tools that turn up when the ground on Forty Oaks is plowed or dug. Their trading partners provided roughed-in 'blanks' that the local peoples could finish. In return, they would have provided corn or perhaps tobacco and other agricultural products. Under pressure from the colonists, they may have joined in the Yamasee War of 1715 which at one point threated the continuation of the colony of South Carolina. Following the defeat of the Native Americans in 1717, these tribes shriveled in size and eventually merged into the Catawba tribe, which remains in existence today.

Slavery & the Civil War:  Many Trees was built prior to the Civil War, most likely in 1857 by Fendol Southerland as his home as he built out a cotton plantation on some newly acquired land. Southerland owned 32 slaves who planted, harvested, and processed the cotton. In addition, they probably cleared the land of timber and built the main house, barn, and other outbuildings. As a part of their work, they also built a giant wheel that powered a cotton gin to process the raw cotton and a giant press to form it into bales. The wheel still stands in the barn on the property and is pictured on the left. By the start of the Civil War, Many Trees was one of the three largest cotton producers in Orange County.

Many Trees stands near the site of some of the last combat in the Civil War. Following their defeat at Bentonville, Confederate troops retreated through the area. Confederate cavalry deployed to protect their line of march along the line of the nearby New Hope Creek. On April 15, 1865, Union cavalry attacked across the New Hope in the vicinity of Many Trees. After four hours of fighting, the Confederates withdrew, perhaps saving Many Trees from being burned as many plantations were late in the war. The Confederates surrendered three days later in nearby Durham, effectively ending the war.

After the Civil War:  Southerland remained on his former plantation and continued growing cotton. In the late 1870s, growing global competition significantly depressed prices from pre-Civil War levels. For planters who lived on credit, as most did, this was disastrous. Southerland was among those affected. In 1878, in failing health, he committed suicide. Around this time, the main house would have looked like the picture to the left. Southerland's grandaughter continued farming the land for decades until the early 1900s. Between 1909 and 1940, the property was owned by a series of land and timber companies. Many of the fields disappeared under new growth forest and the buildings crumbled. In 1940, the Mayor of Chapel Hill, John xxxxxxx, as well as his wife, Ola Maie, bought the property and began to restore it. They lived on the farm for more than fifty years

Today:  Many Trees is owned today by June xxxxx, the daugher of Ola Maie and John. She recalls playing in the fields as a child, turning up arrowheads like those pictured on this page. June has continued her parents' work restoring the farm, rebuilding the barn from a state of near collapse to where it appears as it would have during the time of slavery.
